Parasocial print media

Before TV, muscular heroes would appear episodically on the newsstands and dominate mass media. They were more accessible and engaging than books and exploded in popularity during the 1940s and 1950s.

The heroes presented a persona in possession of things the audience, typically young boys desire: muscular strength, courage, and power.

Squint your eyes and it‚Äôs a podcast about MMA.

Readers quickly bonded to their favorite hero and habitually returned to buy the latest issue to resolve the cliffhanger from the previous episode.

The heroes‚Äô world-saving optimism was a source of hope amidst a society recovering from the great depression.

Parasocial interactions before mass media

Before mass media, parasocial relationships were fostered through ceremonies, routine, and religious worship. The media personas of this era were gods, heroes, prophets, philosophers, and kings.

The masses were told stories of these personas from speakers in close physical proximity.

Jesus blowing up his follower count during the Sermon on the Mount.

The greeks had gods such as Zeus, Athena, and heroes like Hercules. A rich menagerie of personas appealed to and created bonds with the audiences and became interwoven into the social order.

Eastern cultures produced devotion to personas such as the Hindu deities which due to their religious-based interactions, hold cultural relevance to this day.

Reconciling my feelings for Joe Rogan

We return to 2022, where I sit and watch clips of Joe and his comedian friends.

Are parasocial relationships good or bad?

At one end of the spectrum, it is entertainment, a way to relax. On the other end, the consumption of personas strengthen a coping strategy that restrains positive action to improve your situation.

In my case the answer (projection) is clear:

I need to get off my iPad, leave my apartment and socialize with people at the local comedy club.
